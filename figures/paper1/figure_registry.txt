Two-panel figure: intact/effective co-occurrence + texture dot plot.

Panel A: 2×2 heatmap of Intact × Vegetation response co-occurrence.
  N = 743 berms. φ = -0.01, p = 0.739  (no significant association).
  Off-diagonal (discordant) cells highlighted in red.

Panel B: Proportion with vegetation response by soil texture and berm
  condition (Intact vs Degraded). Top 3 textures: slope_200, slope_100, surf_sandbg.
  Textures with significant intact/degraded difference: Sandy loam (significant).

Statistics:
  Panel A — φ (phi coefficient) measures association between two binary
  variables; equivalent to Pearson r for 2×2 tables. φ = 0 indicates no
  association; ±1 indicates perfect association. p-value from Pearson χ²
  test (N = 743).
  Panel B — proportions compared between Intact and Degraded berms within
  each texture class using two-sided Fisher's exact test. Bold solid lines
  connect pairs with p < 0.05; dashed lines are non-significant.
  p-values annotated on significant pairs.

Interpretation:
  Panel A: berm intactness and vegetation response are nearly independent
  (φ = -0.01, p = 0.739). The near-zero φ indicates that whether a berm
  is intact or degraded bears little overall relationship to whether it
  produces a vegetation response.
  Panel B: among the top 3 soil textures, only Sandy loam
  shows a significant intact/degraded difference (p = 0.034). Intact berms
  on Sandy loam have a notably higher proportion
  of vegetation response than degraded berms. Finer-textured soils (clay
  loam, silt loam) show no significant difference, possibly because higher
  water-holding capacity reduces the marginal benefit of berm intactness.

Figure 3 — Berm condition (Intact vs Degraded) by predictor class (2×2 panel)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Four grouped bar panels showing the proportion of berms classified as Intact
vs Degraded, stratified by: (A) berm length class (Long / Short, threshold
60 m), (B) slope class (Shallow ≤2% / Steep >2%), (C) landform (fan
terrace, flood plain, stream terrace), and (D) soil texture (classes with
n > 100).

Significance brackets use one-sided Fisher's exact tests for two-category
panels and pairwise Fisher's exact for multi-category panels; overall
chi-square p-values are shown in panel titles.

Display mode: proportion

Key test results:
  Condition – Intact: Long (>60 m)=47.1% vs Short (<60 m)=70.4%, p=***
  Condition – Degraded: Long (>60 m)=52.9% vs Short (<60 m)=29.6%, p=***
  Slope – Intact: Shallow (≤ 2%)=63.3% vs Steep (> 2%)=55.0%, p=*
  Slope – Degraded: Shallow (≤ 2%)=36.7% vs Steep (> 2%)=45.0%, p=*
  Landform: chi-square overall p = 0.204 (ns)
  Soil texture (n > 100): chi-square overall p = 0.000401 (significant)

Interpretation:
  Berm condition is significantly associated with berm length (shorter berms
  tend to be more intact) and slope class. Landform and soil texture show
  varying degrees of association with condition.

SI Figure 1 — Landform distribution by predictor category (count, dodged bar)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Four panels showing the count of berms in each landform class (Fan terraces,
Flood plains, Stream terraces) split by: berm length class, slope class,
clay content (high/low), and soil development (B horizon / No B horizon).
Significance brackets show two-sided binomial tests (H₀: 50/50 split within
each landform group); labels: *** p<0.001, ** p<0.01, * p<0.05, ns = p≥0.05.

Statistical tests (two-sided binomial, H₀: equal split within landform):

  Berm length (Short (≤ 50 m) vs Long (> 50 m)):
    Fan terraces: n=276  Short (≤ 50 m)=110 (40%), Long (> 50 m)=166 (60%)  p=0.0009 ***
    Stream terraces: n=140  Short (≤ 50 m)=71 (51%), Long (> 50 m)=69 (49%)  p=0.9327 ns
    Flood plains: n=359  Short (≤ 50 m)=146 (41%), Long (> 50 m)=213 (59%)  p=0.0005 ***

  Slope class (Shallow (≤ 2%) vs Steep (> 2%)):
    Fan terraces: n=276  Shallow (≤ 2%)=36 (13%), Steep (> 2%)=240 (87%)  p=0.0000 ***
    Stream terraces: n=140  Shallow (≤ 2%)=109 (78%), Steep (> 2%)=31 (22%)  p=0.0000 ***
    Flood plains: n=359  Shallow (≤ 2%)=239 (67%), Steep (> 2%)=120 (33%)  p=0.0000 ***

  Clay content (Low clay vs High clay):
    Fan terraces: n=276  Low clay=215 (78%), High clay=61 (22%)  p=0.0000 ***
    Stream terraces: n=140  Low clay=140 (100%), High clay=0 (0%)  p=0.0000 ***
    Flood plains: n=359  Low clay=76 (21%), High clay=283 (79%)  p=0.0000 ***

  Soil development (No B horizon vs B horizon):
    Fan terraces: n=276  No B horizon=13 (5%), B horizon=263 (95%)  p=0.0000 ***
    Stream terraces: n=140  No B horizon=124 (89%), B horizon=16 (11%)  p=0.0000 ***
    Flood plains: n=359  No B horizon=349 (97%), B horizon=10 (3%)  p=0.0000 ***

Interpretation:
  Berm length: Long berms predominate on flood plains and fan terraces;
    stream terraces show roughly equal proportions (ns), suggesting that
    landform does not strongly constrain berm length at that setting.
  Slope class: Steep slopes are far more common on fan terraces and flood
    plains than on stream terraces, consistent with the geomorphic gradient
    from active alluvial fans to low-gradient stream corridors.
  Clay content: High-clay soils concentrate on flood plains; fan terraces
    and stream terraces are predominantly low-clay — reflecting the finer
    sediment deposited in flood-plain environments.
  Soil development: B-horizon soils dominate fan terraces and stream
    terraces, while flood plains are largely No-B-horizon, indicating younger
    or more frequently reworked surfaces on the flood plain.
  Overall: Predictors are non-randomly distributed across landforms,
    motivating their inclusion as covariates and supporting landform as a
    meaningful stratification variable in the main analysis.
